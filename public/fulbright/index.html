My parents emigrated from Mexico in 1954 and settled near downtown Los Angeles.  The neighborhood and nearby public schools were gang-ridden, so my father—wanting the best for us—sacrificed greatly to send us to private school.  My childhood had its usual ups and downs, and I did well in school.  However, during my junior year of high school, I experienced a traumatic life-changing event.  While driving with my family to Mexico for a wedding, our brand new Ford Bronco had a blowout and rolled over.  My 2-year-old niece and I were in a coma for a week.  When I emerged from the coma, doctors at Scripps Hospital immediately performed brain surgery.  I was paralyzed on the left side and had a broken pelvis, ribs, clavicle, and skull, so I had to learn how to walk all over again.  I recovered and left the hospital two months later.  Thereafter, my family filed a lawsuit against the auto company—at that time, one of the first lawsuits for faulty tires.  However, the ordeal would take five years and prove to be fruitless—a devastating experience, both emotionally and financially for me and my family.

After graduating from high school, I enrolled at San Diego State University.  I was determined to overcome all obstacles and become the first in my family to graduate from college.  While attending classes at California State University, Long Beach, I took a semester off from school to go to court with my family. The jury trial lasted 12 weeks.  Things were going very well for us, and we were confident we would win the case.  However, at the end of the trial, the judge suddenly changed the jury’s instructions making it impossible for us to win.  My family and I lost the lawsuit and received nothing for our medical expenses.  I came to the painful realization that justice is not always served.  Justice often exists only for those who can afford it.  Truth can become a casualty against corruption and greed. Regardless, I had to set aside my family’s loss and the injustice we had suffered, and continue to excel in my journalism studies. 

In 1991, after receiving my bachelor's degree in journalism/public relations (with a minor in Spanish) from CSULB, I began my first job as program director for the American Language Center with UCLA.  Soon after, I became the first webmaster at Caltech.  There was only one website in existence at the time, which was the Super Computer Center at Urbana-Champaign (creators of Mosaic). 

In those early days, the concept of FTP and coding HTML using vi on a UNIX machine was intensely difficult, but I managed to teach myself everything I needed to know to build a website.  Within one month's time, I created the first site for the Geological & Planetary Sciences Division.  I later redesigned it, and created a second site, this time for the Seismological Laboratory. 

I designed logos and building signage.  To raise funds from alumni, I was asked to design the first Caltech Alumni Newsletter for the GPS Division.   Later, I trained staff members across campus how to write code and design a newsletter and website for their division.  To sharpen my skills further, I took several courses at Art Center College of Design including photography, package design, Photoshop, Quark, and Illustrator.  I attended national web seminars. 

After five years at Caltech, I found my niche, what I loved to do most— DESIGN.   Before leaving Caltech, I was offered a design job at the San Jose Mercury News in the Silicon Valley.  However, once I moved, I was told that the design position at the SJMN was terminated.   Nonetheless, I did some consulting work for them and worked temporarily at Netscape. 

I then moved closer to the coast and secured a position as a webmaster/designer at the San Francisco Unified School District's Language Academy.  Eventually, I returned to Southern California-to be closer to my family-and worked at several IPO's.  I also continued my own design business, consulting for other departments at Caltech.

Then began one of the most exciting times of my life!  From January to July of 2002, I traveled through Europe, visiting Sweden, France, Italy, Holland, Spain, Greece, Germany, and Switzerland. During that time, I developed designs for a few European businesses, one of which is Entergate Technologies in Sweden.  In Madrid, I rented an apartment for several months and developed a great love and appreciation for my Spanish heritage and culture.  I enjoyed very much exploring my Basque roots in Bilbao and San Sebastian.

I visited several Spanish museums—the Dali museum in Figueras, the Prado Museum in Madrid, and the Picasso museums in Malaga and Barcelona—and found them to be profoundly inspiring and full of rich culture.  All of these experiences, as well as my experiences with the people, filled me with fresh creative insights, innovative ideas, and more intimate perspectives.
When I returned from Europe, I continued my consulting business and worked at Foxsports.com as photo editor.  I moved to Huntsville, Alabama last year in search of better design opportunities.  I am currently designing for a large printing company called White Tiger Printing.  As Webmaster/Art Director I have created for them a new identity branding campaign, a new website, and designed ads, folders, stationary, brochures, and calendars.

The past 13 years have flown by.  And I'm asking myself once again what I really want to do with my life.  I half-answered the question long ago when I knew my talents lay in the field of design.  But I also know that I love to work with people, those who have great talent and those with great potential.  These realizations have led me to the next step, one that excites me and motivates me—I want to teach!  I want to share the things I've learned and the experiences I've had.  I have learned to recognize talent in those I've worked with over the years, and I want to cultivate those gifts and talents in young people who are just getting started.

I want to go back and earn a master’s degree in Graphic Design in the one place where I felt the most personal connection and inspiration-Spain. I LOVED Barcelona!  The culture, art, architecture and people were amazing.  But I also feel a tremendous responsibility to immerse myself in another culture as I gain more education.  As the world learns to communicate more and more on a global level, it becomes the responsibility of those who help facilitate that communication, artists and designers included, to do it well.  I want to be one of those people.  My personal life experiences have given me compassion for those who are underprivileged and need to be heard.  And my professional life experiences have given me the expertise and proficiency to give a voice to those who need it. I ask the Fulbright committee to consider my application for a scholarship-and make one of the most precious dreams of my life come true.

Sincerely,
Beatrice Aispuro
